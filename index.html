RENOIR project

Reverse EngiNeering of sOcial Information pRocessing (RENOIR) is EU Horizon2020 Project in the framework of Marie Skłodowska-Curie Actions Research and Innovation Staff Exchange (RISE) Call: H2020-MSCA-RISE-2015.

Project goals

The general research aim of the Project is to reverse-engineer information dynamics in social networks, such as spreading of innovations and rumours among people, viral spreading of information in social media such as Twitter or Facebook and dynamics of news and their topics across time. The Project’s objectives are: discovery and reverse-engineering the mechanisms of information spreading in social media, such as dynamics of news releases, blog and internet for posts, Twitter messages, e-mails etc., training and exchange of knowledge between partners in different domains coming from Warsaw University of Technology, Jozef Stefan Institute, Wroclaw University of Science and Technology and leading world universities Stanford University, Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, Nanyang Technological University, University of California, Carnegie Mellon University Northeastern University, Notre Dame University and University of Technology Sydney bidirectional knowledge transfer between academia and media industry (Slovenian Press Agency) by exposing researchers to real-life problems and giving business access to innovative methods and tools for information analysis. The project will be based on three pillars: data acquisition, data mining/machine learning and complex systems modelling. The specific problems addressed will include understanding rules of and predicting information spreading in different media and about different topics, finding information sources and uncovering hidden information channels. The secondments will accelerate the individual careers of involved researchers, especially early-stage ones. The project will lay foundations for long-term collaboration by strengthening existing links between partners and creating new ones.

Schedule

Time Speaker Title
10:00 Przemyslaw Kazienko Temporal Social Networks through the Prism of Cognition
Abstract: Human relations are driven by social events - people interact, exchange information, share knowledge and emotions, or gather news from mass media. These events leave traces in human memory. The initial strength of a trace depends on cognitive factors such as emotions or attention span. Each trace continuously weakens over time unless another related event activity strengthens it. Here, we introduce a novel Cognition-driven Social Network (CogSNet) model that accounts for cognitive aspects of social perception and explicitly represents human memory dynamics. For validation, we apply our model to NetSense data on social interactions among university students. The results show that CogSNet significantly improves quality of modeling of human interactions in social networks.
11:00 Michalina Kotyla Quantification of relationship strength in temporal social network
Abstract: Human relations nowadays are determined by a lot of factors. Mobile phones and social media are the alternative for face to face contact. All of that is generating huge amount of communication data. Are we able to base on the communication data to determine who is in stronger relationship with each other? Using NetSense dataset about communication data between students of University of Notre Dame in this presentation I will show the try for analysis the relationship strength. Three different approaches to choosing the representative subset of data were examined: k–last events, k–last days, sliding window.
11:30 Coffee break
12:00 Stanislaw Saganowski Group Evolution Prediction in complex networks
Abstract: The presentation will cover the most common approach to group evolution prediction – dividing data set into time windows, detecting communities, matching alike communities, calculating their metrics, and finally applying machine learning tools and techniques to perform the prediction. The presentation will also include comments and guidelines that will help you undertake group evolution prediction with your data set. Additionally, possible research directions in this area will be provided.
13:00 Rajmund Kleminski Topic structures of science
Abstract: An analytic look into the nature of scientific inquiry follows the directions and patterns present within the body of scientific work, expressed through the ideas turned into published papers. As the fields of research deepen and specialize, the interconnected structure of research papers provides less and less information about the grand state of science at large to both the casual observer and the veteran of the field. This increasingly incremental, fine-grained progress in an ever broader set of fields necessitates dedicated approaches towards summarizing the landscape of science in a human-digestible format. In this presentation, we will discuss the motivations and use-cases behind models capable of solving such a problem. Equally important, we will consider the limitations and inherent problems faced when attempting to extract the topic structure of science.
13:30 Lunch
14:00 Tomasz Kajdanowicz Machine learning meets complex networks: efficient estimation of centrality measures and applied graph embedding for dynamic networks
Abstract: With the recently observed wide use of information technologies, complex networks are becoming increasingly popular to capture relationships across various disciplines. Among others, it includes social networks, scientific and citation networks, telecommunication networks, and biological networks. Analysis of such networks gives a new perspective on describing phenomena that govern social life such as the structure of societies, information diffusion, and communication patterns. However, the large-scale network data often makes analytics computationally expensive or intractable. During the talk, I will present my recent findings obtained in a collaboration with Prof. Nitesh Chawla (Notre Dame, USA) and Prof. Mikolaj Morzy (PUT, Poland), that addresses the problem of node importance assessment in the network as well as efficient node embedding in dynamic networks. More specifically, I will present a surprising finding we discovered, that it is possible to estimate the betweenness of a vertex very accurately based solely on the energy of the egocentric network of the vertex. This is a very promising result because it allows skipping the most expensive step of betweenness computation, namely, finding the shortest paths between all pairs of vertices in the network. Then I will elaborate on our new proposal for node embedding in dynamic networks that accomplishes the process in an incremental manner, allowing to keep the network representation up to date at low computational cost.
15:00 Kamil Tagowski Incremental embedding of nodes in dynamic graphs environment
Abstract: Graph representations became an integral component of solving many graph-related problems like link prediction, node classification. In the environment of dynamic graphs, where the structure of the graph evolves (appearance of new nodes, new edges) is crucial to handle these changes. To handle this problem, we propose ICMEN (Incremental Convex Meta Embedding for Nodes) method which can merge information from embeddings calculated on graphs snapshots instead of recalculating the whole representation. In this talk, I will present the idea of ICMEN method, motivations and performance of this method on real-world datasets.
15:30 Closing remarks
